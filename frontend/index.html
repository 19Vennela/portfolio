Slurrp Farm & Mille · Founder's Office Intern · January – April 2025.

I joined Wholsum Foods expecting to learn how products are built. Instead, I found myself asking much bigger questions: Why should a child keep an empty milkshake can? Can the colour of a pancake come from the grain itself? Should breakfast always be sweet? Can a warehouse visit solve a digital problem?

Over three months, I worked across founder's office initiatives that ranged from new product development and packaging strategy to business analytics, warehouse operations and company-wide systems — learning how hundreds of small decisions shape the products that eventually reach supermarket shelves.

🌾 New product development

Building Products Before They Exist

Worked on early-stage product exploration across multiple categories — Strawberry Pancake Mix, Savoury Pancakes, Korean Noodles, Milkshake Mix, Cookies, Veg Chips, Lentil Chips and Crackers.

Rather than designing products, I was trusted to question whether they should exist at all — benchmarking competitors, studying consumer behaviour, exploring portfolio fit, pitching concepts to leadership and asking what genuine problem each product solved.

🍓 A case study within the case study

Turning Research into Decisions

One idea I particularly enjoyed exploring was whether a Strawberry Pancake Mix could tell its story through its ingredients rather than artificial colour.

After benchmarking products across India and internationally, I proposed using red millet (lal jowar) to naturally support the characteristic pink colour while strengthening the millet narrative — a concept that eventually moved into R&D trials.

This project taught me that product innovation isn't about coming up with ideas — it's about backing them with research.

Alongside this, I explored packaging formats, reusable concepts and sustainability opportunities — including ideas that started years earlier when I had written to Slurrp Farm as a customer about unnecessary plastic packaging.

UX Design Intern · July – October 2025.

My internship at Ericsson was my first experience working on an enterprise UX product used internally across a global organisation. Unlike consumer products where success is often measured by engagement, this project focused on understanding how people actually worked — observing their workflows, uncovering hidden frustrations, and designing around real behaviours rather than assumptions.

Over three months, I redesigned Ericsson's internal GenAI Playground by following the complete UX process — from research planning and user interviews to synthesis, prototyping and stakeholder presentations.

🔍 The wall of questions

Research Before Solutions

One of the first lessons I learnt was not to fall in love with solutions too early.

Instead of opening Figma immediately, I spent the first phase understanding the product, defining research goals, identifying success metrics, planning interviews and deciding what needed to be learnt before any interface could be redesigned.

This shifted my perspective from "How do I improve this screen?" to "Am I solving the right problem?"

🎙️ Field notes

Conversations That Changed the Design

One of the most rewarding parts of the internship was conducting user interviews across different teams and roles.

What began as an opportunity to observe interviews quickly turned into leading them myself. Through conversations with designers, developers, HR teams and international users, I learnt that the most valuable insights rarely came from asking what people wanted — they came from watching how they actually worked.

Rather than collecting opinions, I focused on understanding behaviours, pain points and moments of friction.

🧩 Affinity mapping

Making Sense of Complexity

Research was only the beginning.

Every interview, observation and usability issue had to be organised into something meaningful. Using affinity mapping, feature categorisation and structured synthesis, I grouped recurring patterns into actionable design opportunities — separating UX issues from UI concerns, technical limitations and content problems before deciding what deserved attention.

This stage taught me that good UX isn't finding answers quickly; it's making sense of ambiguity.

✏️ Sketches → Figma

From Ideas to Interfaces

Only after understanding the problem space did I begin designing.

The process moved from sketching and Crazy 8s to information architecture, wireframes and finally high-fidelity prototypes in Figma.

Every design decision could be traced back to research rather than intuition, making iteration feel intentional rather than subjective.

🤖 Behind the Playground

Sharing What I Was Learning

Towards the end of the internship, I had the opportunity to conduct an internal knowledge-sharing session introducing designers to Generative AI, Large Language Models, tokenisation and prompt engineering.

Instead of treating AI as a black box, I wanted to explain why these systems behave the way they do — and how understanding concepts like tokenisation could help designers create better AI experiences.

The presentation became one of my favourite moments of the internship because it combined technology with storytelling in a way that felt accessible and engaging.

🌿 Reflection

What I Took Away

Before Ericsson, I thought UX was primarily about designing interfaces.

This internship taught me that the interface is often the smallest part of the work.

The real challenge lies in understanding people, asking better questions, embracing uncertainty and allowing research — not assumptions — to shape every design decision.
